 In the above picture you can notice the differences in words over time as English progressed and evolved as a language. English has changed an enormous amount over this period of time, from new words from other languages being adopted, to some words changing in meaning, to even large word order and grammatical changes. English is typically defined by these three major periods due to the rapid change taking place within the language from the 12th to 15th century. Old English is thought to have been defined by the settling of the first Anglo-Saxon peoples in what is now considered England.


Old English and Middle English

 English at this time still retained many elements from its Germanic roots, but was at least partially initially influenced in several ways by the existing Celtic peoples in the area at the time. "Beowulf" is a famous example of a work we have written in Old English. Below you can see a small piece of Old English and its translation. The "æ" character can be thought of as the sound of the letter A in the word "at." The "þ" and "ð" are both used depending on context for the two respective sounds we have in English currently for the "th" combination of letters (like the "th" in "the" and the "th" in "breath"). The grammar of Old English also contained elements not seen in grammar in modern English, like 3 grammatical genders for nouns and agreeing adjectives, and also different forms of nouns depending on the various roles they play in the sentence. Verb conjugations also generally had more forms than we know today.

 During the period of Old English, two separate groups at different times invaded and influenced the language in different ways. The first were Scandinavian and spoke Old Norse, which is considered a North Germanic language and is the precursor to languages such as Norwegian and Icelandic. They altered the language in numerous ways, including "simplifying" the grammar by reducing the forms of verbs. The second group were from modern day northern France and spoke Old French, and contributed a great deal to the current vocabulary of Modern English along with other changes. This occupation took place during the end of Old English throughout Middle english. When the ties were severed with northern France in the beginning of the 13th Century, the nobility in England gradually became English and English speaking, but before this period French was considered the language of nobility and English was the language for the common people. "The Canterbury Tales" is an example of something written during this time.
